Marcus's Design Principles

Key Principles

  • Visibility
  • Consistency
  • Feedback
  • Constraints
  • Affordances
  • Accessibility

Complexity Considerations

  • Tesler's Law: The Law of Conservation of Complexity
    • For any system, there is a certain amount of complexity that cannot be reduced.
  • "As Easy as Email":
    • Allow users to learn by doing instead of tutorials
    • Help users adapt more efficient methods - in-context learning, tooltips, etc.
    • Provide fluid pathways
    • Reduce clutter without losing complexity
    • Make important information visually salient

Emotional/Accessibility Principles

  • Ortony's model of emotional design consists of three levels:
    • Visceral - the events of the physical world
    • Behavioral - the brain processes that control everyday actions
    • Reflective - the brain processes involved in contemplating

Human Centered Design Principles

  • Puts human needs, capabilities, and behavior first
  • Design to meet those needs

Affordances vs Signifiers

  • Affordance: a clue that an action can be performed
  • Signifier: a guide/direction to help users use an affordance

The Seven Stages of Action

  1. What do I want to accomplish?
  2. What are the alternative action sequences?
  3. What action can I take now?
  5. What happened?
  7. Is this OK?
  • Provide discoverability
  • Provide feedback
  • Utilize a conceptual model
  • Include affordances, signifiers and constraints
  • Ensure you have good mappings - the relationship between controls and their actions

Cultural/Gender Considerations

  • The Seven Dimensions of Culture:
    1. Individualism vs communitarianism
    2. Universalism vs peculiarism
    3. Specific vs diffuse
    4. Neutral vs emotional
    5. Achievement vs absorption
    6. Sequential time vs synchronous
    7. Internal vs outer direction

Conceptual Considerations

  • The five main types of a user interacting with a product are:
    1. Instructing
    2. Conversing
    3. Manipulating
    4. Exploring
    5. Responding
  • The three components of design are the designer, the user and the system, resulting in three different models of how the system works:
    1. Designer's Model - how the system should work, according to the designer
    2. System Image - how the system actually works
    3. User's Model - how the user believes or understands that the system works

Cognitive Design Principles

  • Attention - selecting things on which to concentrate
    • Consider context.
    • Avoid clutter.
    • Consider designing different ways of supporting effective switching and returning to a particular interface.
  • Perception - how information is acquired from the environment via the senses
    • Design icons to be easily extinguished from one another.
    • Research proper color contrast techniques.
    • Obvious separators and whitespace are helpful to group information.
    • Haptic feedback should be used judiciously.
  • Memory
  • Learning
  • Reading, speaking and listening
  • Problem-solving, planning, reasoning and decision-making

Interface Principles

  • WIMP - Windows, Icons, Menus and Pointing devices
    • The dominant four attributes which categorize the interface developed by Xerox.
  • Designs can take advantage of isomorphism, or similarity to existing things in the world, to help convey metaphors and functions to the user.
